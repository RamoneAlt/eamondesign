Long Term Issues

In this section, we delve into the long-term challenges and concerns associated with the water crisis in East Africa. By understanding and addressing these issues, we can work towards sustainable solutions and secure the future of our water resources. Together, let's navigate the path toward a water-secure future.

Population Growth and Demand

The rapid population growth in East Africa leads to much greater water demand. Countries like Tanzania have a population growth rate of around 3% compared to the U.S.A, which has a rate of only around 0.5%. The fast-rising population stresses already limited water resources, leading to water scarcity and possible conflicts over access to water.

Global Warming

Global warming is tremendously affecting the water crisis in East Africa. Global warming leads to severe water scarcity, flooding, and droughts. The warming is happening faster in Africa than in other parts of the world, with a rate of around 0.3 degrees Celsius or 0.5 degrees Fahrenheit every decade, making the water situation much more challenging. Global warming impacts water availability, quality, and access in East Africa, and those affecting global warming the most are more developed countries like the United States.

Effects of Global Warming

  • Rising sea levels. A direct cause of rising sea levels is more flooding and an increased salinity (salt) in groundwater.
  • Failing rainy seasons. East Africa has recently faced many failed rainy seasons in numerous regions, which will worsen as times progress.
  • Extreme weather. Global warming leads to harsher weather conditions such as flooding, droughts, and dry monsoons.
  • Glaciers melting at a faster rate. Glaciers in Mount Kenya, Kenya, Mount Kilimanjaro, Tanzania, and the Rwenzoris Mountains, Uganda, are retreating faster than the global average glacial retreating rate.

Higher Poverty Rates

Roughly 65% of Africa's population relies on subsistence farming, a practice of growing just enough to live until the next harvest. However, due to a higher amount of failed rainy seasons and droughts in the future, agriculture will continue to take strong blows leaving many communities vulnerable, which can escalate poverty rates further into the future.
